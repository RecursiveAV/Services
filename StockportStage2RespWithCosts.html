01. Where we are

Main Summary

Stockport is undergoing an exciting £1billion transformation programme and the latest phase is now being unveiled in Merseyway. This is to be the main arrival point for the town.

The Stockroom is a radical new proposal which will create a new arrival point for the town centre, breathing new life into empty retail spaces. It is a ‘learning and discovery store’ which will inspire, entertain and support the people of Stockport and bring vibrancy and animation to the Town Centre, better supporting our town’s shops and businesses.

It will be a place to celebrate the rich culture, creativity and diversity of Stockport, a place where the past, present and future come together. A place to spend time exploring with friends and family, children and grandchildren, parents and grandparents and a place to share knowledge and exchange ideas. A place where people come together to connect, learn and grow.

This document explores the digital elements which will be used to further enhance the user journey across the spaces, joining up spaces and unifying the experience, whilst providing rich information to the visitors of the space, allowing them to discover and engage in the many services and experiences which Stockroom will provide.

The Stockroom is split over two floors and incorporates the original Adlington Walk digital scheme previously proposed. The following layouts are the latest work in progress interior designs, courtesy of Space Invader Design which highlight the key areas where digital may feature.

02. Creative & Technological Challenges

With the many exciting opportunities Stockroom presents comes an equal number of challenges, mainly in the form of designing a cohesive approach to the Digital elements across these multiple different areas.

We must ensure that we have an approach which joins together the distinct areas of the Stockroom project, forming a common language and providing information to visitors in a clear form, in order to enhance their experience and ensure we achieve repeat visits and a growing audience. Some of the challenges for us to solve are:



•  

The incorporation of the existing Adlington Walk digital scheme into the new Stockroom concept

•  

The incorporation of the new services including Library, Museum/Discovery Centre, Registrar & Council Services, Event Spaces, Health and Well-Being and Educational spaces

•  

The holistic approach to digital and content lead elements of the scheme which provides an approach which is common to all areas and allows for engagement

•  

The provision of information and the enrichment of experience through content both playful and immersive

•  

Managing the budget of introducing these new elements to ensure that costs are controlled and carefully balanced against the benefits of these digital additions

03. An Overarching Motif

A Unifying Theme

In order to solve these challenges and join up the spaces across the Stockroom it will be essential to have an overarching theme that allows the content and digital approach to each space to be linked together.

This approach should also drive footfall and engagement and immersion, creating a real journey that invites visitors to explore all the areas and also learn about what is on offer at the Stockroom both permanently and on a rotational basis for example events and educational programs.

This theme needs to be flexible, translating across the spaces. It needs to be able to connect them together in a holistic way. It also needs to be appropriate for all age groups, based on something which will appeal to both young and old.

In essence this theme needs to be something which floats over the surface of the core offering of services across the Stockroom being both engaging and playful. If we get this theme right, this will also potentially open the opportunity for branding, merchandising or other forms of revenue generation.

03/01

Interaction & Engagement

Initial Thoughts For A Theme

To initialise the brainstorming process, we started off by thinking about the interactivity and engagement we wish to achieve, that could act as a common language regardless of the services in areas in which it was found. We need something that could work both individually but also in groups, that will allow for a wider content driven take over across multiple digital elements of the space, opening up the idea of community and event driven content.

Initially we explored abstract thinking, however, reviewing the thoughts and ideas of Space Invader Design (SID) for the interior inspiration and looking at how nature forms such a strong element of this, we felt that this needed to be something which would fit directly into this theme and something that would evoke emotions and connections with the audience.

03/02

Nature and Living Systems

Birds as a unifying theme

Based on this concept of nature we have decided to focus on the use of an animal/creature, one which strongly features in both countryside and towns and that evokes memories and emotions. Following this logical path we have arrived at the concept of the use of birds, which are colourful, intelligent, widespread, and are a part of every culture and religion used to unify the space across all areas. We believe this approach crosses all boundaries in terms of age and theme and that the theme of birds can relate to all areas in different ways.

Our proposal is to work with real birds, especially local ones, however this can also expand to the more exotic. This doesn’t exclude the idea that we can invent our own for the purposes of this project. Our aim is to show birds across the space in a number of surprising ways, sometimes deliberately in juxtaposition with the surrounding environment. Birds will be displayed at different times and at different places across the space, appearing across the digital real estate installed, but also appearing in unexpected locations through the use of projection.

Different birds can be seen each week or month as part of the voyage of discovery, encouraging a bird spotting competition - collect them all, including some which are rare and can only be sighted on certain day(s) & times. Similar approaches have been used to great success in schemes across UK towns, such as the snowdogs in Ashford and Brighton.

These schemes have reliably proven the idea that such an approach drives people around the space to visit all areas, which they might not normally do, thus discovering the services and facilities as a result of this journey. A checklist will be created to drive this journey so that each week more birds can be spotted and collected. This checklist could be in the form of a mobile app, a HTML microsite or could also be in printed form. If in paper form, this could be handed out at the information desk…If in digital form this be built in as a key feature of the hublets/digital devices, to allow access to all.

To further promote accessibility and also to promote engagement, we also propose to have a couple of “digital periscopes” installed at key locations within the Stockroom space. These “Digital Periscopes” will allow users to pan around the space and spot the birds - whilst also calling out key Library and Discovery Centre features through On Screen Display overlays which acts as a mini wayfinding/service guide for the Stockroom. These Periscopes could also take the form of binoculars or perhaps these could even be deployed inside a bird hide or a tree house, which would be perfect for the children’s area. The hide could also feature cushions and bean bags, creating a comfortable place for younger children to gather.

03/03

Thinking Digitally

BOID Systems

The movement of birds is an interesting scientific area, and has been modelled mathematically through a process known as "Boids". Boids is an artificial life program, developed by Craig Reynolds in 1986, which simulates the flocking behaviour of birds. His paper on this topic was published in 1987 in the proceedings of the ACM SIGGRAPH conference. The name "boid" corresponds to a shortened version of "bird-oid object", which refers to a bird-like object.

As with most artificial life simulations, Boids is an example of emergent behaviour; that is, the complexity of Boids arises from the interaction of individual agents (the boids, in this case) adhering to a set of simple rules. The rules applied in the simplest Boids world are as follows: separation: steer to avoid crowding local flockmates alignment: steer towards the average heading of local flockmates cohesion: steer to move towards the average position (center of mass) of local flockmates. More complex rules can be added, such as obstacle avoidance and goal seeking.

03/04

Digital Adaptation

Eviolution of the BOID Systems.

By making use of digital approaches, combined with real life imagery, we believe we can arrive at a unified approach which creates a highly engaging and reactive content environment across the Stockroom, making use of this theme across all digital elements which are proposed.

This digital approach to nature based themes allows us greater freedom to adapt and drive content in real time, based on people's movements through the space, their direct interactions with exhibits and screens, plus leveraging mobile based interactions to drive our data driven systems. This approach will be unique to the Stockroom and will draw an audience to experience the forever changing content.

04/01

Proposed Technogies

Technologies

The following section is an overview of the types of technologies that would be suitable for deployment across the Stockroom. These have been selected to drive content and user engagement, whilst providing content rich information in support of the services on offer.

04/01

Mobile based applications

AR, QR Codes, Microsites

Mobile based applications, particularly in the form of Augmented Reality (AR) driven content, feels like a perfect companion for the Stockroom, allowing an expansion of content triggered through the physical elements present in the space.

Augmented Reality allows hidden/virtual elements to be revealed and overlaid onto the physical environment in real time, based on the coordinates of the person's position at the viewpoint of the camera within their mobile device.

AR can be used to steer people through the space revealing wayfinding details and calling out areas of the stock room for people to explore. For example can you show the positions of genres of books within the library through hovering text base callouts over the physical shelves when viewed through the mobile device. In the museum it can be used to bring to life exhibits showing content rich information around the history of the object.

AR can also be used to bring our overarching theme to life allowing a bird watching based feature allowing tracking of the birds across the library who may be perched amongst the books on shelves in the Library or Museum or indeed roosting above the cafe counter.

Coasters, cups and serviettes can be printed with unique tracking markers which will allow birds to be revealed through AR across the tables of the cafe, bringing delight to young and old.

Regular weekly or monthly bird spotting treasure trails can be provided encouraging users to track down and add to their mobile bird spotting book all of their sightings.

04/02

PAINTING WITH LIGHT

Projection and Projection Mapping

We envisage that projection and projection mapping will be used throughout space in order to create elements of surprise and playfulness, adding a sense of theatre. Projection remains a magical medium due to its ability to be turned off and on and indeed faded between these two states, or driven through user interaction and movement.

It also integrates perfectly with the fabric of the building without any need or intrusive works using the surfaces of walls, ceilings, floors and indeed furniture, as the canvas. It can also be integrated as part of the wider lighting scheme treated as another lighting element to create ambience and a sense of changing aesthetic, based on time of day, weather conditions, seasons and so on.

04/03

Dynamic Projection

Projector with Mirror Head

As an enhancement to standard projection, the use of a projection moving mirror head allows a cost-effective way to create a larger canvas area that may be covered by a single projector. The projections can be moved up, down, left and right in real time to reach certain angles and locations that are not in the projection standard range if they were statically positioned.

When combined with content which is created to work with this movement, truly magical and engaging experiences can be built, all from a single projector.

04/04

SHARED EXPERIENCES

Big Screen Augmented Reality

Big Screen AR is a mixture of the two previous technologies, where the augmented content is shown to a larger audience. This type of approach is found to be particularly engaging in public spaces due to the fact it removes the need for any technology to be used by anyone to make this work (i.e a mobile).

It also allows large scale interaction and audience observation which encourages community engagement and conversation. It is also highly shareable, which is good for social channels. The large screen in Adlington walk would be the perfect place for this.

Tablets

Digital Tablets can be used as portable devices to allow digital engagement by visitors. This is already the central part of the library through the use of hublets. As part of the scheme it should be explored whether tablets have a wider use particularly for visitors who may wish to engage in signing up for Council services for example.

Tablets can also be used to allow access to mobile-based applications which may be running engaging content throughout the Stockroom such as AR based applications which overlay content digitally into the built environment. 04/07

TRADITIONAL DISPLAYS

Digital Displays Cases

For Museum exhibits which need to be kept under controlled conditions, we propose the use of digital display cases which allow graphical content and text-based information to appear floating over and in front of the objects to inform the visitor of the history of the item on display. Content can be triggered through simple presence detection which will sense the proximity of the visit to the case changing the content from an attraction mode, designed to catch the attention of visitors and draw them over to the case, through to detailed information once their local presence to the object is sensed.

Content can easily be changed and updated based on a change of object, making these cases a flexible way of security displaying artefacts whilst providing content rich information.

Smaller cases can be used to display smaller artefacts in the same way or used to show pure digital versions of elements from the archive in a true 3D representation. Content could be driven through a simple touchscreen or tablet which would allow the user to select an item to view in 3D. Content could be curated in support of an exhibition which is running in the museum/discovery centre to allow additional objects in relation to the exhibition to be shown for which there may not be physical space.

A more compact approach could also be taken for small objects, through the integration of a grid based case system which is installed. This physical display is then driven in tandem with rich digital content which explains the history, importance and heritage of each piece.

04/08

3D Solutions

Holographic Displays

Compact holographic display cases could also be employed. These work by proving the illusion of 3D floating content overlaid onto a real object which is housed within the case.

Content can be triggered based on proximity to allow an attractor sequence to draw people in to investigate. Once close to the case, the content can change to provide call outs and overlays which bring the object to life.

04/09

TOUCHLESS INTERACTIVITY

Motion Sensing and Interactive Sensors

Motion Sensors

Motion sensors such as the Kinect Azure or the Orbbec Astra can be deployed to track the movement of people and drive content based on this. This allows both passive and interactive engagement around content tracking the user's position in 3D space, using these coordinates to drive content in reaction to this in real time.

Interactive Projection

A Projection table can be used to allow content to be overlaid on physical objects which respond to a user’s touch in a simple format. This type of approach is favoured over and involves a traditional touch screen or touch table as the user interface is very simple and removes barriers from users who may be intimidated, and it also allows physical elements to be displayed and amplified and uses standard furniture, such as an ordinary table top which is both tactile and somewhat magical.

04/10

PHYSICAL INTERACTIONS

Connecting Digital with the physical

Physical interactions can be used to drive digital experiences creating an interesting mix of real and digital (so called phygital) which provides strong engagement from users. Such interactions could include button pushes or lifting levers, moving squares or shapes. All should be aimed at encouraging users to engage with the physical offerings across the Stockroom.

By way of example, within the library the action of pulling certain books out from a shelf could drive a light sensor or roller switch which senses that movement. This in turn would drive a small animation which moves over the bookshelves - this could be a bookworm for example. The animation could be randomised meaning it would not be the same each time.

As a further play on the overarching theme, this bookwork could at times attract the birds who want to eat it (but perhaps never manage it) adding a greater sense of playfulness and engagement.

As a further example, “Bird seed” could be purchased from the cafe which could be in the form of edible fruit or sweets of a certain kind which when held in the open palm of the hand would attract virtual birds to land and feed from this using the AR application. This “Bird Seed” would be a good example of a revenue generating element which could come from activities which can only be experienced within the Stockroom.

04/11

digital preservation

Photogrammetry

Photogrammetry is the art and science of extracting 3D information from photographs. The process involves taking overlapping photographs of an object, structure or space and converting them into 2D or 3D digital models.

We believe that Photogrammetry will be extremely useful to the Stockroom project in allowing artefacts from the museum's collection to be digitised and then displayed across the digital elements suggested within this document.

The Recursive team are able to assist the Museum staff in setting up this process in order to generate content for the Stockroom's launch, and subsequent rolling curation programs.

By digitising large amounts of physical objects and artifacts, the council will achieve a steady stream of new and unique digital content. This can also then be used online and through other channels to advertise the Stockroom and the project.

05/03

Performance Space (Ground Floor)

The Forum performance space will host a wider range of public facing talks, presentations, demonstrations and debates. We will need to support these activities through a series of digital hardware which needs to be compact and easy to use for everyday use. We feel that there is a wider opportunity here to generate content output from this space to grow the audience of the Stockroom and therefore suggest that both streaming and recording of events is also included. The following hardware and software elements should be considered:

Projection Wall

The wall behind the presenter's position shall be used as a large projection surface, eradicating the need for a dedicated projection screen, subject to a suitable paint colour (white through to grey) and a suitable matt finish. The wall space is fairly large, which opens up the opportunity to explore an ultra wide screen projection, allowing background and foreground content to be displayed. This approach will be more immersive for the audience, and will look more professional to an external audience, who may be joining a live stream, or perhaps may watch this as a recording online.

A single camera would be sufficient to enable this service, however as a further enhancement, it may be interesting to explore the use of an overhead camera option for demonstrations so that close ups can be shown to both the local audience on the projection wall, as well as the online audience.

A curtain based masking system could be considered so that the aspect ratio of the projection surface can shift from single 16:9 mode to an ultra widescreen mode depending on event type, presenter and audience. In order to control costs this masking system could be manual, with simple wall hung drapes on rails.

Recording/Streaming

Both recording and live streaming services will allow for a larger audience to participate and create the possibilities of hybrid events, which are becoming ever popular. The content which is created from this space can drive a new area of the council’s website allowing remote viewers to watch live or later.

A single camera would be sufficient to enable this service, however as a further enhancement, it may be interesting to explore the use of an overhead camera option for demonstrations so that close ups can be shown to both the local audience on the projection wall, as well as the online audience.

Connectivity

Basic events will need a laptop connection so that content can be shown via the projector. We would suggest both a wired and wireless connection is deployed to ensure flexibility plus a fallback should one fail.

Audio

As the space is relatively small and compact, audio support requirements will be limited. Voice reinforcement may be needed, but in the true sense i.e not load amplification, but a subtle natural uplift to ensure all audience members can adequately hear the presenter.

If this is the case then we would recommend ceiling mounted speakers positioned over the audience seating area.

We will also need to support media playback and this should be done via a permanently installed set of compact wall mounted speakers, positioned to the left and right of the projection surface.

Acoustics

In order to control the acoustics of the space and provide some isolation and a sense of intimacy, It would be useful to consider the use of wool serge drapes which would pull around the back of the seats in a semi circle, following the shape of the seating units.

These would hang from floor to ceiling and would be manually operated to create an enclosed area which gives a sense of privacy and also better lighting control providing a better lighting environment for the projector and allowing some small spot lights to be used to highlight the presenter/guest speaker for a more professional look.

Equipment Rack

Space will need to be found to house a small rack with the kit to support this space. This could be in the form of a compact roll around rack, which could be free standing, or this could be incorporated into a lectern or into the seating unit construction.

If the latter is adopted this would offer the option of a technicians position which could be pulled out from the side of seating.

The external balcony will be used for large events and for public hire, such as weddings, or large gatherings. We envisage that the majority of equipment will be hired in for events, this being a broad range of lighting, projection and audio. Provision needs to be made therefore, for this equipment to be connected as follows:

•  

Wireless and Wired Network coverage. Wired Network may be used to provide signal distribution for AV to external temporary projection. Wired network sockets to be IP rated.
